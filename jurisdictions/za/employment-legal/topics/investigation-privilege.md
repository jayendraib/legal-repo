# Investigation and Privilege — SA Privilege, Protected Disclosures, POPIA

South African overlay for internal investigation and privilege skills. Covers legal professional privilege under SA law, the in-house counsel distinction, the Protected Disclosures Act, POPIA considerations during investigations, and SA-specific procedural adjustments.

---

## SA legal professional privilege

### Common law foundation

South African legal professional privilege derives from the **English common law tradition** and is recognised as part of the common law of South Africa. It protects confidential communications between a client and a legal practitioner from compelled disclosure in legal proceedings.

There are two forms:

### Legal advice privilege

Confidential communications between a client and an admitted **attorney** or **advocate** (or, after the Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014, a legal practitioner) made for the **dominant purpose of obtaining or giving legal advice** are privileged.

Requirements:

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Admitted legal practitioner** | The communication must be with a person admitted and enrolled as an attorney, advocate, or legal practitioner under the Legal Practice Act |
| **Client relationship** | There must be a professional client-legal practitioner relationship (not merely a commercial or collegial one) |
| **Dominant purpose: legal advice** | The communication must be made for the dominant purpose of obtaining or providing legal advice. Communications with mixed purposes (business and legal) are assessed on whether the legal advice purpose dominates |
| **Confidentiality** | The communication must have been made in confidence and must not have been voluntarily disclosed to third parties outside the privileged relationship |

### Litigation privilege

Communications and documents created for the **dominant purpose of pending or contemplated litigation** are privileged. This extends beyond communications with legal practitioners to include communications with third parties (e.g., expert witnesses, investigators) made in preparation for litigation.

Requirements:

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Litigation in contemplation or pending** | Litigation must be reasonably in prospect — not merely speculative. A CCMA referral, Labour Court proceeding, or arbitration qualifies. |
| **Dominant purpose** | The document or communication must have been created with the dominant purpose of use in the litigation (not merely incidentally relevant to it) |
| **Extends to third-party communications** | Unlike legal advice privilege, litigation privilege covers communications with non-lawyers (investigators, witnesses, experts) made in preparation for the litigation |

## What is NOT protected

South African legal professional privilege is **narrower** than the attorney-client privilege and work product doctrine in some other jurisdictions. The following are generally NOT protected:

| Category | Why not privileged |
|---|---|
| **Internal analysis by in-house counsel acting in a commercial/management capacity** | Privilege attaches to the legal advice function, not to the person. When in-house counsel acts as a business adviser, manager, or commercial decision-maker, the communications are not privileged. |
| **Documents in the ordinary course of business** | A document created in the ordinary course of business (e.g., a routine compliance report, an HR memo, a policy draft) is not privileged merely because it was copied to a lawyer or because it touches on legal issues. |
| **Communications with non-lawyers** | Communications with non-lawyers (HR, management, compliance) are not covered by legal advice privilege, even if those communications discuss legal issues — unless they form part of a chain to or from a legal practitioner for the purpose of obtaining or giving legal advice, or they fall under litigation privilege. |
| **Pre-existing documents** | A document that existed before the privileged relationship or litigation was contemplated does not become privileged by being sent to a lawyer. Privilege protects the communication, not the underlying facts. |
| **Waived privilege** | Privilege is waived by voluntary disclosure to a person outside the privileged relationship. Partial disclosure of a privileged document may waive privilege over the whole document. |

## In-house counsel distinction

### The Euroshipping principle

The South African position on in-house counsel privilege is informed by *Euroshipping Corporation of Monrovia v Minister of Agricultural Economics and Marketing* (1979) 1 SA 637 (C), which drew a distinction between an in-house legal adviser acting **as a legal adviser** and the same person acting **as a member of the management team**.

### Practical implications

| Role | Privilege position |
|---|---|
| **Acting as legal adviser** — providing legal advice on a specific legal question, instructing external counsel, advising on litigation strategy, reviewing contracts for legal risk | Communications in this capacity are capable of attracting legal advice privilege |
| **Acting as management team member** — attending management meetings in a general capacity, making business decisions, drafting commercial proposals, participating in operational planning | Communications in this capacity are NOT privileged, even if the person is an admitted legal practitioner |

### Practical guidance for investigations

- **Identify the capacity clearly.** When in-house counsel is involved in an investigation, distinguish between communications made in the capacity of legal adviser and those made in a management or HR capacity.
- **Mark privileged communications.** Use a header such as *"PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL — COMMUNICATION FOR THE PURPOSE OF LEGAL ADVICE"* — but note that the header is a label, not a control. Privilege depends on the substance of the communication, not the label.
- **Separate legal advice from factual findings.** An investigation report that mixes factual findings with legal advice may lose privilege over the entire document if the factual portions are not severable. Consider producing a separate legal advice memorandum.
- **Instruct external counsel where privilege is critical.** Where the investigation may lead to litigation (CCMA, Labour Court, or civil proceedings), consider instructing external counsel to conduct or oversee the investigation. This strengthens the privilege position.

## Protected Disclosures Act 26 of 2000

### Overview

The Protected Disclosures Act 26 of 2000 (PDA) protects employees who make disclosures of information about improprieties in the workplace from **occupational detriment**. The PDA is South Africa's whistleblower protection statute.

### Key principles during an investigation

| Principle | Obligation |
|---|---|
| **No victimisation** | An employee who makes a protected disclosure may not be subjected to any occupational detriment by the employer. Occupational detriment includes disciplinary action, dismissal, suspension, demotion, harassment, intimidation, transfer against the employee's will, refusal of transfer or promotion, and any other adverse treatment. |
| **Preserve confidentiality** | The identity of the discloser must be protected to the extent reasonably possible. Investigation findings should not reveal the discloser's identity without consent. Where the disclosure leads to a disciplinary hearing, the employer must consider how to protect the discloser while still ensuring procedural fairness for the respondent. |
| **Do not use the investigation to build a case against the discloser** | An investigation triggered by a protected disclosure must investigate the substance of the disclosure — not the discloser's conduct, motives, or performance. Using the investigation as a pretext to gather information to discipline or dismiss the discloser is a form of occupational detriment. |
| **Good faith is not required (post-2017 amendment)** | Following the Protected Disclosures Amendment Act 5 of 2017, the requirement of good faith was removed. A disclosure is protected if the employee reasonably believes that the information is substantially true, regardless of motive. |

### What constitutes a protected disclosure

A disclosure of information regarding any **impropriety** committed or about to be committed by the employer or an employee, including:

- Criminal offences.
- Failure to comply with a legal obligation.
- Miscarriage of justice.
- Endangerment of health or safety.
- Environmental damage.
- Unfair discrimination.

The disclosure must be made through one of the channels recognised by the PDA: to a legal adviser, to the employer, to a member of the Cabinet or Executive Council, to a prescribed body, or as a general protected disclosure (subject to additional requirements).

### Investigation flags — Protected Disclosures

| Flag | Risk | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Investigation subject recently made a complaint about the employer | Possible protected disclosure — victimisation risk | Assess whether the complaint qualifies as a protected disclosure before proceeding. Do not allow the investigation to be or appear to be retaliatory. |
| Discloser's identity is at risk of exposure | PDA breach, victimisation claim | Implement measures to protect identity. Consider anonymous reporting channels. |
| Investigation scope has expanded to include the discloser's conduct | Appearance of reprisal | Separate the investigation of the disclosed impropriety from any separate investigation of the discloser's conduct. Document the independent basis for any action against the discloser. |

## POPIA considerations

### Processing employee personal information during investigations

The Protection of Personal Information Act 4 of 2013 (POPIA) governs the processing of personal information during workplace investigations. Key considerations:

### Lawful basis for processing

An employer conducting an internal investigation may rely on one or more of the following lawful bases:

| Basis | POPIA reference | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Legitimate interests** | POPIA s11(1)(f) | The employer's legitimate interest in investigating misconduct, provided it does not infringe the employee's right to privacy disproportionately. A balancing test applies. |
| **Legal obligation** | POPIA s11(1)(c) | Where the employer is legally obliged to investigate (e.g., OHSA incident reporting, EEA compliance, or a bargaining council requirement). |
| **Compliance with the law** | POPIA s11(1)(b) | Processing necessary for compliance with an obligation imposed by law (broader than a specific legal duty). |

Consent (POPIA s11(1)(a)) is generally **not the appropriate basis** for processing employee personal information during an investigation, because the power imbalance in the employment relationship means consent is unlikely to be freely given.

### POPIA principles during investigations

| Principle | POPIA reference | Practical application |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose limitation** | s13 | Process personal information only for the purpose of the investigation. Do not use information gathered for the investigation for unrelated purposes (e.g., performance management, general surveillance). |
| **Minimality** | s10 | Collect only the personal information that is necessary and relevant to the investigation. Do not conduct a broad trawl of an employee's records beyond what the investigation requires. |
| **Notification** | s18 | The data subject (employee being investigated) must be notified that their personal information is being processed — **unless** the investigation exemption applies: notification may be delayed where it would prejudice the investigation (POPIA s18(4)(b): processing is necessary for the enforcement of the law, or it would prejudice a lawful purpose of the collection). |
| **Security** | s19 | Investigation materials (documents, witness statements, recordings) must be secured against unauthorised access. Restrict access to those involved in the investigation. |
| **Retention** | s14 | Do not retain investigation records indefinitely. Consider CCMA referral timelines (30 days for unfair dismissal, 6 months for unfair labour practice) plus a reasonable buffer for litigation. A retention period of 3 years from the conclusion of the investigation or the resolution of any related dispute is common practice. |

### Investigation exemption for notification (POPIA s18(4)(b))

Where notifying the subject of the investigation would **prejudice the purpose of the investigation** (e.g., risk of evidence destruction, witness intimidation, or flight), the employer may delay notification. However:

- The delay must be justifiable and proportionate.
- Notification should be provided as soon as the prejudice risk passes.
- The employer must document the reasons for the delay.

## SA-specific investigation adjustments

### Right to representation

In a disciplinary hearing (as distinct from the investigation stage), the employee has the right to be represented by a **fellow employee** or a **trade union representative** (LRA Schedule 8 Item 4(1)). Key points:

| Aspect | SA position |
|---|---|
| **Who may represent** | A fellow employee of the employer, or a trade union representative (shop steward or official of the employee's trade union). |
| **External legal representation** | There is **no automatic right** to external legal representation at a disciplinary hearing. External legal representation is available only if the employer's disciplinary code provides for it, or if the chairperson exercises a discretion to allow it (factors: complexity of the matter, whether the employer is legally represented, the seriousness of the charges, the employee's ability to present their own case). |
| **During investigation (pre-hearing)** | There is no statutory right to representation during the investigation stage (fact-gathering interviews). Best practice is to allow a union representative to accompany the employee in an investigation interview, but this is not mandatory. |

### CCMA evidentiary standard

When an investigation may lead to a CCMA referral (unfair dismissal, unfair labour practice), the employer should be aware of the CCMA's evidentiary approach:

| Feature | CCMA approach |
|---|---|
| **Formality** | Less formal than a court — no strict rules of evidence. The commissioner has a duty to deal with the substantial merits of the dispute with the minimum of legal formalities (LRA s138(1)). |
| **Hearsay** | Admissible, but carries **less weight** than direct evidence. A commissioner will consider hearsay but may require corroboration before relying on it. |
| **Standard of proof** | **Balance of probabilities** — not beyond reasonable doubt. The employer must show that it is more probable than not that the employee committed the misconduct. |
| **Documentary evidence** | The employer should prepare a bundle of documents. Originals are preferred; copies are admissible but may be challenged. |
| **Video/audio recordings** | Admissible subject to authentication. RICA (Regulation of Interception of Communications and Provision of Communication-Related Information Act 70 of 2002) governs interception — a party to a communication may record it, but interception of a communication to which you are not a party requires a court order. |

### Privilege log — narrower scope

Because South African legal professional privilege is narrower than in some other jurisdictions, a privilege log in the context of a South African investigation will typically contain **fewer documents** than an equivalent log in a jurisdiction with a broader work product doctrine.

| Document type | Likely privileged? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Legal advice from external attorney/advocate on the investigation | **Yes** | Legal advice privilege — dominant purpose is legal advice |
| Internal memo from in-house counsel providing legal advice on the investigation | **Likely yes** | Provided in-house counsel is acting in a legal advisory capacity, not a management capacity |
| Factual investigation report (prepared by HR, not counsel) | **No** | Not a communication for legal advice; not prepared for dominant purpose of litigation (unless litigation is already contemplated) |
| Witness statements gathered by investigators | **Possibly** | Privileged only if gathered for the dominant purpose of litigation that is pending or contemplated (litigation privilege) |
| Management discussion notes on how to respond to findings | **No** | Business/management communications, not legal advice |
| Email chain between in-house counsel (acting as legal adviser) and HR seeking legal guidance on the investigation | **Likely yes** | The chain is for the purpose of obtaining legal advice; privilege should extend to the HR person's communications that are necessary to enable the lawyer to provide informed advice |
| Pre-existing documents (attendance records, CCTV footage, emails between employees) | **No** | Pre-existing documents do not become privileged by being collected for an investigation |

### Investigation timeline awareness

When conducting an investigation that may result in disciplinary action and potential dismissal, keep the following timelines in mind:

| Timeline | Detail | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| CCMA referral for unfair dismissal | **30 days** from date of dismissal | LRA s191(1) |
| CCMA referral for unfair labour practice | **90 days** from the act or omission, or from the date the employee became aware of it | LRA s191(1) |
| Late referral (condonation) | The CCMA may condone a late referral on good cause shown | LRA s191(2) |
| Record retention (recommended) | **3 years** from conclusion of investigation or resolution of related dispute | Best practice, informed by CCMA/Labour Court limitation periods and POPIA retention principle |
