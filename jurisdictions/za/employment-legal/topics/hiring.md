# Hiring — Restraint of Trade, Probation, EEA at Hire

South African overlay for hiring-related skills. Covers restraint of trade enforceability, probation under the LRA, Employment Equity Act obligations at recruitment, and employment contract requirements under the BCEA.

---

## Restraint of trade

### Constitutional foundation

Section 22 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996 guarantees every citizen the right to choose their trade, occupation, or profession freely. A restraint of trade clause limits this right. South African courts will enforce a restraint only where the limitation is reasonable in the circumstances.

### The Basson v Chilwan four-factor test

The leading authority is *Basson v Chilwan* (1993) 3 SA 742 (A). The Appellate Division (now Supreme Court of Appeal) established four factors, all of which must be satisfied for enforcement:

| Factor | Question | Practical test |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Protectable interest** | Does the employer have a proprietary interest deserving of protection? | Trade secrets, confidential information, trade connections/customer relationships, workforce stability. General skill and experience are NOT protectable. |
| **2. Reasonable scope** | Is the restraint reasonably necessary to protect that interest — in geographic area, duration, and scope of activity? | A nationwide 2-year blanket restraint on a junior employee is unlikely to survive. A 12-month non-solicitation of named clients may. |
| **3. Reasonable between the parties** | Weighing up the interests of both parties, is enforcement fair? | Consider: seniority, bargaining power at signing, whether the employee had independent legal advice, whether consideration was given for the restraint. |
| **4. Not contrary to public interest** | Would enforcement harm the public interest? | Rarely determinative on its own, but relevant where restraint would deprive a community of a scarce professional (e.g., a specialist doctor in a rural area). |

The onus is on the **employee** to show that the restraint is unreasonable — *Magna Alloys and Research (SA) (Pty) Ltd v Ellis* (1984) 4 SA 874 (A). However, courts have moved toward a more balanced assessment: *Reddy v Siemens Telecommunications (Pty) Ltd* (2007) 2 SA 486 (SCA).

### Judicial remedies — severance and reading down

South African courts may:

- **Sever** an unreasonable portion of a restraint and enforce the remainder, provided the clause is structured to permit severance (e.g., separate sub-clauses for duration, geography, and scope).
- **Read down** an overbroad restraint to what would be reasonable — though courts are less willing to rewrite a poorly drafted clause than to sever a clearly separable portion.

### Non-solicitation vs non-compete

Non-solicitation clauses (restricting contact with specific clients or employees) are **more readily enforced** than blanket non-compete clauses (restricting the employee from working in an entire industry or for any competitor). Employers should prefer targeted non-solicitation where possible.

### Review checklist — restraint of trade at hire

- [ ] Is there a protectable interest (trade secrets, confidential information, customer connections)?
- [ ] Is the duration reasonable for the role and seniority (6-12 months is common; 24 months is at the outer edge)?
- [ ] Is the geographic scope no wider than necessary?
- [ ] Is the scope of restricted activity specific and proportionate?
- [ ] Is the clause structured for severance (separate sub-clauses)?
- [ ] Was the restraint signed at commencement of employment (or was fresh consideration provided for a mid-employment restraint)?
- [ ] Did the employee have a reasonable opportunity to obtain independent legal advice?
- [ ] Is the restraint from a previous employer — if so, assess enforceability before making an offer that may trigger litigation.

## Probation

### LRA Schedule 8 Item 8

Probation under South African law is governed by LRA Schedule 8, Item 8 (Code of Good Practice: Dismissal). Probation is **not a free pass** to dismiss without process. The statutory requirements during probation are:

1. **Evaluation** — the employer must assess the employee's performance against clear, communicated standards.
2. **Guidance and instruction** — the employer must provide the employee with the guidance and support necessary to perform.
3. **Training** — where performance shortfalls relate to skills gaps, the employer must provide appropriate training.
4. **Counselling** — the employer must counsel the employee about areas of underperformance and explain what improvement is expected.
5. **Reasonable opportunity to improve** — the employee must be given a fair chance to meet the required standard before dismissal is considered.
6. **Extension before dismissal** — consider extending the probation period before deciding to dismiss.

### Fair procedure during probation

A probationary dismissal must still comply with the requirements of procedural fairness, although the process may be less formal than for a confirmed employee (Schedule 8 Item 8(1)(i)). The employee must be:

- Informed of the performance concerns.
- Given an opportunity to respond.
- Heard before a decision is made.

A CCMA commissioner will assess whether the employer met these requirements. Dismissal during probation without any process remains procedurally unfair.

### Common probation periods

| Duration | Typical use |
|---|---|
| 3 months | Standard for most roles |
| 6 months | Senior or specialist roles, roles requiring longer learning curves |
| Beyond 6 months | Unusual — requires justification; extended probation should be formally agreed and documented |

### Probation flags

| Flag | Risk | Action |
|---|---|---|
| No documented evaluation during probation | Procedural unfairness at CCMA | Implement structured check-ins before any dismissal decision |
| Dismissal without counselling or opportunity to improve | Substantive and procedural unfairness | Provide counselling, set targets, allow time to improve |
| Probation period expired, employee still working | Employee is now confirmed — probation protections no longer apply, full Schedule 8 process required | Confirm status formally or extend probation in writing before expiry |
| Successive short fixed-term contracts used to avoid probation | CCMA may treat as single continuous employment | Use a proper probation clause in a permanent contract instead |

## EEA obligations at hire

### Designated employer threshold

Under EEA s1, an employer with **50 or more employees** is a designated employer and must comply with the affirmative action provisions of Chapter III of the Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998.

### EE plan targets inform hiring

A designated employer must prepare and implement an employment equity plan (EEA s20). The EE plan sets numerical goals and timetables for achieving equitable representation across occupational levels. Hiring decisions should be informed by the EE plan targets for the relevant occupational category and level.

This does not mean that an underqualified candidate must be appointed, but where two candidates are equally qualified, the EE plan targets are a lawful consideration in the selection decision.

### Psychometric testing (EEA s8)

Psychometric testing and other similar assessments of an employee are prohibited unless the test or assessment being used:

1. Has been **scientifically shown to be valid and reliable**.
2. Can be **applied fairly to all employees**.
3. Is **not biased** against any employee or group.

Employers must be able to demonstrate that any pre-employment psychometric assessment meets all three requirements. The burden is on the employer.

### Pre-employment medical testing (EEA s7)

Medical testing of an employee is prohibited unless:

- **Legislation requires** the testing (e.g., occupational health requirements for specific industries under the Mine Health and Safety Act 29 of 1996 or the Occupational Health and Safety Act 85 of 1993).
- It is **justifiable** given the inherent requirements of the job (e.g., a pilot's medical fitness).

Medical testing must be authorised by the employer's own policies and must comply with the requirements of the Act.

### HIV testing (EEA s7(2))

Testing of an employee for HIV is **prohibited** except where authorised by the **Labour Court** on application. The court must be satisfied that the testing is justifiable, taking into account:

- The employment equity plan.
- Medical facts.
- Employment conditions.
- The inherent requirements of the job.
- Social policy.
- The fair distribution of employee benefits.

An employer cannot unilaterally require an HIV test as a condition of employment.

### Hiring review checklist — EEA compliance

- [ ] Is the employer a designated employer (50+ employees)?
- [ ] Have EE plan targets been considered for this position?
- [ ] Are any psychometric tests validated, fair, and unbiased per EEA s8?
- [ ] Is any pre-employment medical testing legally required or justified by inherent job requirements per EEA s7?
- [ ] Has HIV testing been excluded (or Labour Court authorisation obtained)?
- [ ] Does the job advertisement avoid unfair discrimination on listed grounds (EEA s6)?
- [ ] Is the selection process documented to demonstrate compliance if challenged?

## Employment contract requirements

### BCEA s29 — written particulars

The BCEA s29 requires the employer to supply the employee with written particulars of employment **when the employee commences employment**. The written particulars must include:

| Particular | Reference |
|---|---|
| Full name and address of the employer | BCEA s29(1)(a) |
| Name and occupation of the employee, or a brief description of the work | BCEA s29(1)(b) |
| Place of work (or required travel) | BCEA s29(1)(c) |
| Date employment began | BCEA s29(1)(d) |
| Ordinary hours of work and days of work | BCEA s29(1)(e) |
| Wage or rate of remuneration, method of calculation, and rate of pay for overtime | BCEA s29(1)(f)-(h) |
| Frequency of remuneration payment | BCEA s29(1)(i) |
| Deductions from remuneration | BCEA s29(1)(j) |
| Leave entitlement | BCEA s29(1)(k) |
| Notice period required for termination | BCEA s29(1)(l) |
| Description of any council or sectoral determination that covers the employer's business | BCEA s29(1)(m) |
| Period of employment (if not indefinite) | BCEA s29(1)(n) |
| Any other prescribed particulars | BCEA s29(1)(o) |

### Timing

Written particulars must be supplied on or before the first day of employment. Failure to comply is an offence under the BCEA and may result in a compliance order from a labour inspector.

### Practical notes

- The written particulars requirement applies regardless of the employee's earnings level.
- A formal employment contract that contains all the required particulars satisfies s29 — a separate "particulars" document is not required.
- Where a bargaining council agreement or sectoral determination applies, the contract must reference it and may not provide terms less favourable than the applicable minimum.
- Include the restraint of trade clause (if any) in the employment contract itself — restraints in separate side-letters signed after commencement may face enforceability challenges.
