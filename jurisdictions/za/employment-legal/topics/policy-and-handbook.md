# Policy and Handbook — SA Policy Conventions

South African overlay for policy-drafting and handbook skills. Covers the statutory framework governing workplace policies, mandatory and recommended policies, and SA-specific drafting conventions.

---

## SA policy framework

### Statutory floor

South African employment policies operate within a layered statutory framework. There is no concept of "state supplements" — the national statutes set the floor and are supplemented by bargaining council agreements and sectoral determinations where applicable.

| Layer | Source | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Constitution** | Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996 | Fundamental rights (s23 fair labour practices, s22 freedom of trade, s9 equality, s10 dignity, s14 privacy) |
| **National statutes** | LRA, BCEA, EEA, OHSA, POPIA, COIDA, UIA, Skills Development Act, National Minimum Wage Act | Minimum standards applicable to all employers nationwide |
| **Bargaining council agreements** | Negotiated between employer organisations and trade unions for a sector/area, extended by Minister | Sector-specific conditions — may be more favourable than BCEA; binding on all employers in scope once extended |
| **Sectoral determinations** | Issued by the Minister of Employment and Labour for sectors without bargaining councils | Minimum conditions for specific sectors (domestic workers, farm workers, hospitality, contract cleaning, etc.) |
| **Collective agreements** | Between employer and recognised trade union(s) at the enterprise level | Workplace-specific terms negotiated with the union |
| **Employment contract** | Between employer and individual employee | Must meet or exceed statutory and collective minimums |
| **Employer policies** | Unilateral employer policies (subject to consultation where unions are present) | Supplement the statutory framework; must not conflict with legislation or collective agreements |

### Relationship between layers

- A policy may **not** provide terms less favourable than the applicable statutory floor, bargaining council agreement, or sectoral determination.
- Where a bargaining council agreement applies, it **overrides** any conflicting term in the employment contract or policy (unless the contract is more favourable to the employee).
- An employer must check whether a bargaining council agreement or sectoral determination applies before drafting policies — the applicable council or determination may prescribe specific procedures (e.g., disciplinary procedures, shift arrangements, leave provisions) that the employer must follow.

## Mandatory and recommended policies

### Disciplinary code and procedure (essential)

A well-drafted disciplinary code aligned to **LRA Schedule 8** (Code of Good Practice: Dismissal) is the single most important employment policy for a South African employer. CCMA commissioners measure the fairness of dismissals against the employer's own disciplinary code.

#### Structure

| Component | Content |
|---|---|
| **Statement of purpose** | Corrective, not punitive. Progressive discipline aims to change behaviour, not to create grounds for dismissal. |
| **Offence categories** | Table of offences with indicative sanctions. Typically categorised by severity: Category 1 (minor — verbal/written warning), Category 2 (serious — final written warning), Category 3 (very serious — dismissal may be appropriate), Category 4 (gross misconduct — summary dismissal may be appropriate). |
| **Progressive discipline** | Verbal warning, written warning, final written warning, dismissal. Each step requires the offence to be communicated, the employee to be heard, and the sanction to be proportionate. |
| **Disciplinary hearing procedure** | Right to be informed of charges in writing. Right to prepare (reasonable time). Right to be represented by a fellow employee or trade union representative. Right to call witnesses and cross-examine. Right to an impartial chairperson. Right to a written outcome. Right to appeal. |
| **Chairperson** | An impartial person — not the complainant, not the direct manager who investigated, not a person with a personal interest in the outcome. In larger organisations, an independent chairperson from another department or an external chairperson. |
| **Sanction guidelines** | The sanction must be appropriate to the offence, the employee's circumstances (length of service, disciplinary record, personal circumstances), and the employer's circumstances. Consistency with how similar offences have been treated. |
| **Warnings** | Written warnings typically valid for 3-6 months (minor offences) or 6-12 months (serious offences). The policy should specify the validity period. |
| **Appeal** | The employee must have the right to appeal the outcome to a more senior person within a specified period (typically 5 working days). |

#### CCMA alignment

The CCMA assesses both **substantive fairness** (was there a fair reason for the sanction?) and **procedural fairness** (was a fair procedure followed?). Key points:

- An employer who deviates from its own code bears the burden of explaining why.
- The code should be communicated to all employees (induction, training, accessible copy).
- Ignorance of the code may be raised as a defence if the employer has not taken steps to communicate it.

### Grievance procedure (essential)

A clear grievance procedure is essential for resolving workplace disputes before they escalate to the CCMA or a bargaining council.

#### Structure

| Stage | Process | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage 1: Informal** | Employee raises the grievance with their direct supervisor (or the next level if the supervisor is the subject). Discussion and attempted resolution. | 5 working days to respond |
| **Stage 2: Formal (written)** | If unresolved, employee submits a written grievance to HR or the next management level. Investigation conducted. | 10 working days to investigate and respond |
| **Stage 3: Hearing** | If still unresolved, a grievance hearing is convened. The employee may be represented by a fellow employee or trade union representative. | Within 10 working days of referral |
| **Stage 4: Appeal** | The employee may appeal the outcome to a more senior manager or panel. | 5 working days to appeal; decision within 10 working days |
| **Stage 5: External** | If the matter remains unresolved internally, the employee may refer the dispute to the **CCMA** or the applicable **bargaining council** for conciliation. | Within applicable referral timeframes |

### Sexual harassment policy (strongly recommended)

The **Code of Good Practice on the Handling of Sexual Harassment Cases in the Workplace** (issued under the EEA) provides detailed guidance. Employers should adopt a standalone sexual harassment policy that:

- Defines sexual harassment (including verbal, non-verbal, physical, and quid pro quo forms).
- States that sexual harassment is a form of unfair discrimination under EEA s6.
- Provides a complaint procedure separate from the general grievance procedure (to accommodate the sensitivity of complaints).
- Protects the confidentiality of the complainant and the respondent during the process.
- Prohibits victimisation of the complainant for bringing a complaint.
- Provides for interim measures (e.g., separation of parties) pending investigation.
- Specifies possible sanctions, including dismissal for serious cases.
- Commits to awareness training.

### Other recommended policies

| Policy | Governing legislation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Leave policy** | BCEA ss20-27 | Consolidates annual, sick, family responsibility, maternity, parental leave. May provide above-minimum entitlements. |
| **Working hours and overtime** | BCEA ss9-16 | Applicable to employees below the earnings threshold. Include compressed week and shift arrangements if applicable. |
| **Health and safety** | Occupational Health and Safety Act 85 of 1993 (OHSA) | Mandatory H&S representatives (20+ employees), H&S committees (20+ employees at one site), incident reporting, risk assessments. |
| **HIV/AIDS** | Code of Good Practice on Key Aspects of HIV/AIDS and Employment (EEA) | Non-discrimination, confidentiality, no compulsory testing (except with Labour Court authorisation), reasonable accommodation, access to treatment support. |
| **Substance abuse** | OHSA s8 (general duty) | Fitness for duty, testing protocols (where applicable), EAP referral, progressive discipline for policy violations. |
| **IT, email, and social media** | POPIA, RICA, Constitution s14 (privacy) | Acceptable use, monitoring disclosure (RICA s6 requires consent for interception), personal information processing (POPIA), social media conduct guidelines. |
| **POPIA / data protection** | Protection of Personal Information Act 4 of 2013 (POPIA) | Processing of employee personal information, consent and purpose limitation, security safeguards, PAIA manual, information officer registration. |

## Drafting conventions for SA policies

### Reference the controlling statute

Every policy should identify the statute, bargaining council agreement, or sectoral determination it implements. This grounds the policy in law and helps with compliance reviews.

**Example:** *"This Disciplinary Code is issued in terms of, and must be read with, the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, Schedule 8 (Code of Good Practice: Dismissal). Where a bargaining council agreement applies to the employer's operations, the provisions of that agreement prevail to the extent of any inconsistency with this code."*

### Use the BCEA/LRA employee definition

Do not create a bespoke definition of "employee" in a policy. Refer to the statutory definition (BCEA s213 / LRA s213). Where the policy applies differently to employees above and below the BCEA earnings threshold, state this clearly.

### Include acknowledgement forms

Every policy should have an acknowledgement form that the employee signs to confirm receipt and understanding. This is not a legal requirement, but it is critical evidence in disciplinary and CCMA proceedings. The acknowledgement should:

- Identify the policy by name and version.
- State that the employee has received a copy and had the opportunity to ask questions.
- Be dated and signed by the employee.
- Not waive any rights.

### Specify the relationship to bargaining council agreements

If the employer falls within the scope of a bargaining council, the policy should state how it interacts with the council's collective agreement. Typically: *"Where the provisions of the applicable bargaining council agreement differ from this policy, the bargaining council agreement prevails."*

### Include review date and version control

| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| Version number | Sequential (v1.0, v2.0) or date-based (2025-01) |
| Effective date | Date the policy takes effect |
| Review date | Date by which the policy must be reviewed (annually recommended) |
| Approval authority | Name or title of the person who approved the policy |
| Change log | Summary of material changes from the previous version |

### Consultation with trade unions

Where a recognised trade union or workplace forum exists, the employer should consult on policy changes that affect terms and conditions of employment. Unilateral changes to conditions that were previously the subject of collective bargaining may constitute an unfair labour practice (LRA s186(2)).

### Language and accessibility

- Draft policies in plain language accessible to all employees.
- Where the workforce includes employees whose first language is not the language of the policy, consider providing translations or summaries in relevant languages.
- Ensure policies are physically or electronically accessible to all employees, including those without regular computer access.
