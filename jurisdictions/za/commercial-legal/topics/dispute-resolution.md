# Dispute Resolution — South African Framework

This overlay covers SA governing law conventions, arbitration, High Court litigation, prescription, and enforcement of foreign judgments. It is loaded by vendor-agreement-review, saas-msa-review, and nda-review skills when jurisdiction = ZA.

---

## 1. SA governing law conventions

### Default position

SA commercial contracts between SA parties are governed by SA law by default. There is no need to specify SA governing law in a purely domestic contract, but it is standard practice to include a governing law clause for certainty.

### Choice of foreign law

SA law generally respects party autonomy in choosing foreign governing law (English law, New York law, etc.) for commercial contracts. However, SA mandatory law applies **regardless of the parties' choice of governing law** where the contract has a sufficient connection to SA:

| Mandatory SA law | Applies regardless of governing law choice |
|---|---|
| Consumer Protection Act (CPA) | Where the transaction involves a consumer in SA and the goods/services are supplied in SA |
| Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) | Where personal information of SA data subjects is processed |
| Competition Act | Where the conduct has an effect on competition in SA markets |
| Exchange control regulations | Where an SA party makes or receives cross-border payments |
| B-BBEE Act | Where procurement involves organs of state or public entities |
| Labour legislation (LRA, BCEA, EEA) | Where the employment is in SA (employment-legal domain) |

**Practical consequence:** choosing foreign governing law does not allow the parties to contract out of these mandatory provisions. A contract governed by English law between an SA company and a UK vendor that processes personal information of SA data subjects is still subject to POPIA.

### Submission to jurisdiction

SA courts accept submission to foreign jurisdiction clauses in commercial contracts, subject to:

- The submission must be clear and unambiguous
- SA courts retain jurisdiction for matters involving SA mandatory law
- A party that submits to foreign jurisdiction may still approach SA courts for urgent interim relief (interdicts)

## 2. Domestic arbitration

### Arbitration Act 42 of 1965

The Arbitration Act governs domestic arbitration (both parties SA, SA seat). Key features:

- **Binding** — an arbitration agreement is enforceable. A party that commences court proceedings in breach of an arbitration agreement can be stayed (s6).
- **Arbitrator powers** — the arbitrator has the power to determine any dispute arising from or in connection with the agreement, including the existence or validity of the agreement itself.
- **Limited court review** — an arbitral award can be set aside only on narrow grounds: misconduct by the arbitrator, gross irregularity in proceedings, or the award being obtained improperly (s33).
- **No appeal** — unless the arbitration agreement provides for an appeal, there is no right of appeal from an arbitral award.

### AFSA (Arbitration Foundation of Southern Africa)

AFSA is the leading domestic arbitration institution. It administers arbitrations under its own rules, which are based on international best practice.

**Typical AFSA arbitration timeline:**

| Stage | Timeline |
|---|---|
| Filing to appointment of arbitrator | 2-4 weeks |
| Pre-hearing conference | 1-2 weeks after appointment |
| Exchange of statements of claim/defence | 4-6 weeks |
| Document discovery | 2-4 weeks |
| Hearing | 1-5 days (depending on complexity) |
| Award | 4-6 weeks after hearing |
| **Total filing to award** | **12-18 weeks** |

### SCA reaffirmation (2025)

The Supreme Court of Appeal reaffirmed in 2025 that arbitration clauses in commercial contracts are binding and must be given effect. Courts should not permit parties to circumvent arbitration agreements by recharacterising their claims as falling outside the arbitration clause. This strengthens the enforceability of well-drafted arbitration clauses.

### Why arbitration is increasingly preferred

The Johannesburg High Court (Gauteng Division) has experienced significant backlogs, with trial dates extending to **2027** for contested matters. Other divisions (Western Cape, KwaZulu-Natal) have somewhat shorter delays but still face 12-18 month waits for trial dates.

Arbitration offers:

- Faster resolution (12-18 weeks vs 2-3 years in court)
- Confidentiality (court proceedings are generally public)
- Choice of arbitrator (specialist in commercial law)
- Flexibility in procedure (limited discovery, witness statements instead of oral evidence-in-chief)
- Enforceability (limited grounds for court review)

## 3. International arbitration

### International Arbitration Act 15 of 2017

The International Arbitration Act governs cross-border arbitration where:

- One or more parties have their place of business outside SA, OR
- The seat of arbitration is outside SA, OR
- The subject matter of the dispute relates to more than one country

Key features:

- Based on the **UNCITRAL Model Law** on International Commercial Arbitration
- SA courts have limited grounds to refuse recognition and enforcement of foreign arbitral awards (aligned with the New York Convention)
- The Act replaced SA's previous reservation to the New York Convention, making SA a more arbitration-friendly jurisdiction for international commercial disputes

### Practical considerations for cross-border contracts

When drafting arbitration clauses for contracts between SA and foreign parties:

1. **Seat of arbitration** — determines the curial law (procedural law governing the arbitration). An SA seat means SA courts supervise the arbitration; a foreign seat means foreign courts supervise.
2. **Institutional vs ad hoc** — institutional arbitration (ICC, LCIA, AFSA) provides administrative support and established rules. Ad hoc arbitration gives the parties more flexibility but requires more drafting.
3. **Language** — specify the language of the arbitration if parties operate in different languages.
4. **Number of arbitrators** — one for lower-value disputes, three for complex or high-value matters.
5. **Governing law vs arbitration law** — the governing law of the contract (substantive) is separate from the law of the arbitration (procedural). Specify both.

## 4. High Court litigation

### Jurisdiction and divisions

The High Court of South Africa is divided into provincial divisions:

| Division | Seat | Key characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Gauteng Division, Johannesburg | Johannesburg | Largest division; significant backlog; trial dates to 2027 |
| Gauteng Division, Pretoria | Pretoria | Slightly shorter delays than Johannesburg |
| Western Cape Division | Cape Town | Moderate backlog; trial dates typically 12-18 months |
| KwaZulu-Natal Division | Durban / Pietermaritzburg | Moderate backlog |
| Eastern Cape Division | Makhanda / Bhisho / Port Elizabeth | Generally shorter delays |
| Free State Division | Bloemfontein | Generally shorter delays |
| Limpopo Division | Polokwane / Thohoyandou | Shortest delays |
| North West Division | Mahikeng | Shorter delays |
| Northern Cape Division | Kimberley | Shortest delays |
| Mpumalanga Division | Mbombela / Middelburg | Shorter delays |

### Jurisdiction determination

The High Court has jurisdiction where:

- The defendant is domiciled or resident in the division (ratione domicilii)
- The cause of action arose in the division (ratione rei gestae)
- The defendant has consented to the jurisdiction (consent to jurisdiction clause in the contract)
- Property that is the subject of the dispute is in the division (ratione rei sitae)

### Typical litigation timeline (contested matter)

| Stage | Timeline |
|---|---|
| Summons / combined summons | Filing |
| Notice of intention to defend | 10 business days |
| Plea | 20 business days |
| Discovery | 20 business days after close of pleadings |
| Pre-trial conference | Set down by Registrar |
| Trial date allocated | 12-36 months after close of pleadings (varies by division) |
| Trial | 1-10 days (depending on complexity) |
| Judgment | Reserved; delivered 1-6 months after trial |

### Appeals

| Court | Jurisdiction |
|---|---|
| Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) | Appeals from High Court on all matters; leave to appeal required |
| Constitutional Court | Appeals involving constitutional matters; leave to appeal required |

## 5. Prescription Act 68 of 1969

### Prescriptive periods (s11)

Prescription extinguishes the right to institute legal proceedings after the applicable period. The periods relevant to commercial contracts are:

| Claim type | Period |
|---|---|
| Debt (contractual claim for payment of money) | 3 years |
| Delictual (tort) claims for damages | 3 years |
| Other claims (specific performance, interdict, declaratory order) | 6 years |
| Claims against the state | 3 years |
| Mortgage bond or judgment debt | 30 years |

### When prescription begins to run (s12)

Prescription begins to run when the debt becomes **due**. A debt is due when the creditor has knowledge of:

1. The identity of the debtor
2. The facts giving rise to the debt

If the creditor is unaware of the identity of the debtor or the facts giving rise to the debt, prescription is delayed until the creditor acquires (or could reasonably have acquired) that knowledge.

### Interruption of prescription (s15)

Prescription is interrupted by:

- Service of process (summons, application) on the debtor
- Filing of a claim in insolvency or liquidation proceedings
- An acknowledgment of liability by the debtor

Once interrupted, prescription starts running afresh from the date of interruption.

### Contractual limitation periods

Parties may agree to contractual limitation periods that are shorter than the Prescription Act periods. However, the Constitutional Court in **Barkhuizen v Napier** (2007) held that such clauses are enforceable only if they satisfy a two-stage test:

1. **Objective reasonableness** — is the limitation period objectively reasonable, considering the purpose it serves and the context in which it operates?
2. **Subjective fairness** — is it fair to hold this particular party to the clause in the circumstances?

**Practical guideline:** contractual limitation periods of less than 12 months are likely to be challenged. Periods of 6 months or less are high-risk. A clause requiring notification of claims within 30 days of the event (common in insurance contracts) may be unenforceable if the claimant could not reasonably have discovered the claim within that period.

### High-risk flag: Prescription period mismatch

| Check | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Contractual limitation period specified | Does the contract impose a time limit for bringing claims that is shorter than the Prescription Act period? |
| Comparison to statutory period | How does the contractual period compare to the applicable Prescription Act period (3 years for debt, 6 years for other)? |
| Barkhuizen v Napier assessment | Is the shorter period objectively reasonable? Would it be fair to enforce against this party in these circumstances? |
| Discovery trigger | Does the limitation period run from the date of the breach (potentially unfair) or from the date of discovery (more reasonable)? |
| Notice requirements | Are there separate notice-of-claim requirements that could effectively shorten the limitation period further? |
| Reciprocity | Does the shortened period apply equally to both parties, or only to one? |

**Why this matters:** A contractual limitation period that is unconscionably short may be unenforceable under the Barkhuizen v Napier public policy test. If the clause is struck down, the full Prescription Act period applies — which may be longer than the party intended. Conversely, a well-drafted limitation period that is reasonable and reciprocal will be upheld, providing certainty for both parties.

## 6. Enforcement of foreign judgments

### Common law position

SA courts will recognise and enforce foreign judgments under common law, provided:

1. The foreign court had **jurisdiction** according to SA rules of private international law (not merely according to the foreign court's own rules).
2. The judgment is **final and conclusive** — not subject to further appeal in the foreign jurisdiction.
3. The judgment is not contrary to **SA public policy**.
4. The judgment was not obtained by **fraud**.
5. The enforcement would not constitute **double jeopardy** (the same claim already adjudicated in SA).

### Enforcement of Foreign Civil Judgments Act 32 of 1988

This Act provides a statutory mechanism for enforcement of foreign civil judgments from designated countries. However, very few countries have been designated, so the common law remains the primary enforcement route.

### Practical considerations

- **Reciprocity** — SA does not require reciprocity as a condition for enforcement, but the lack of a bilateral treaty means enforcement depends on the common law requirements.
- **Currency** — a foreign money judgment is converted to ZAR at the exchange rate on the date of enforcement.
- **Time limit** — the foreign judgment must be enforced within the applicable Prescription Act period (30 years for a judgment debt).
- **Defences** — the party against whom enforcement is sought can raise the standard defences (no jurisdiction, not final, public policy, fraud).

### Foreign arbitral awards

Foreign arbitral awards are enforced under the International Arbitration Act 15 of 2017, which gives effect to the New York Convention. Enforcement is more straightforward than for foreign court judgments — the court may refuse enforcement only on the narrow grounds specified in the Convention (e.g., the arbitration agreement was invalid, the award deals with matters beyond the scope of the submission, the award has been set aside in the country of origin).

## 7. SA attitude to mandatory law in cross-border disputes

Regardless of the governing law chosen by the parties and regardless of the dispute resolution forum, SA mandatory law applies to SA-connected transactions. The key mandatory regimes are:

1. **CPA** — consumer transactions in SA, regardless of foreign governing law
2. **POPIA** — processing of personal information of SA data subjects, regardless of where the processing occurs
3. **Competition Act** — conduct with an effect on competition in SA markets
4. **Exchange control** — payments by SA parties to non-residents
5. **B-BBEE Act** — government and public entity procurement

An arbitrator (whether sitting in SA or abroad) and a foreign court are expected to give effect to SA mandatory law where the transaction has a sufficient connection to SA. An award or judgment that fails to apply SA mandatory law may face enforcement difficulties in SA on public policy grounds.

## 8. Must-not-contain reference

When jurisdiction = ZA, dispute resolution review skills must not use the following US terms:

| US term | SA replacement |
|---|---|
| "Delaware / New York / California" (as default governing law) | SA law as default; or specified foreign law with mandatory SA law acknowledged |
| "AAA" / "JAMS" | AFSA (domestic) or ICC/LCIA (international) |
| "FRCP" / "Federal Rules of Civil Procedure" | High Court Rules / Uniform Rules of Court |
| "statute of limitations" | prescription (Prescription Act 68 of 1969) |
| "discovery" (US sense — depositions, interrogatories) | SA discovery (limited to documents; no depositions in SA civil procedure) |
| "preliminary injunction" / "TRO" | interdict (interim or final) |
| "forum selection clause" | consent to jurisdiction / prorogation of jurisdiction |
