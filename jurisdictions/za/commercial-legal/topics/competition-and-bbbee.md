# Competition and B-BBEE — South African Framework

This overlay covers the Competition Act restrictive practices regime, the Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act, fronting prohibition, and public sector procurement. It is loaded by vendor-agreement-review and saas-msa-review skills when jurisdiction = ZA.

---

## 1. Competition Act 89 of 1998

The Competition Act regulates restrictive horizontal and vertical practices, abuse of dominance, and mergers. Commercial contracts must be assessed for potential competition law violations, particularly restrictive practices under s4 and s5.

### s4 — restrictive horizontal practices

Section 4 prohibits agreements between competitors (parties in a horizontal relationship) that:

**Per se prohibited (s4(1)(b)):**
- Price-fixing
- Market division (allocating customers, territories, or types of goods/services)
- Collusive tendering (bid rigging)

These are prohibited regardless of market share or competitive effect. There is no efficiency justification defence.

**Rule of reason (s4(1)(a)):**
- Other horizontal agreements that have the **effect of substantially preventing or lessening competition** in a market, unless the parties can demonstrate that the pro-competitive gains outweigh the anti-competitive effects and that the restriction is reasonably necessary to achieve the pro-competitive gains.

### s5 — restrictive vertical practices

Section 5 applies to agreements between parties in a vertical relationship (supplier-customer, franchisor-franchisee, licensor-licensee).

**Rule of reason (s5(1)):**
A vertical agreement is prohibited if it has the **effect of substantially preventing or lessening competition** in a market, unless the parties can demonstrate pro-competitive gains that outweigh anti-competitive effects.

**Per se prohibition — minimum resale price maintenance (s5(2)):**
Section 5(2) prohibits a supplier from requiring or inducing a customer to sell goods or services at a **minimum price**. This is a per se prohibition — no efficiency justification is available.

- Recommended resale prices are permitted, provided they are genuinely advisory and the supplier does not penalise the customer for selling below the recommended price.
- Maximum resale prices are generally permissible unless they amount to price-fixing in effect.

### Penalty for contravention

The Competition Tribunal may impose an administrative penalty of up to **10% of the firm's annual turnover in the Republic** for a first contravention of s4(1)(b) (per se horizontal) or s5(2) (minimum RPM). Repeat offences attract penalties up to **25% of turnover**.

### Exclusivity clauses through the competition lens

Exclusive supply or exclusive distribution arrangements are vertical agreements subject to the s5(1) rule of reason. The analysis considers:

1. **Market definition** — what is the relevant market in which the exclusivity operates?
2. **Market share** — what are the parties' market shares? Higher market shares increase the risk of substantial lessening of competition.
3. **Duration** — long-term exclusivity is more likely to substantially lessen competition than short-term.
4. **Foreclosure** — does the exclusivity foreclose competitors from a significant portion of the market?
5. **Pro-competitive justification** — does the exclusivity promote investment, quality, or innovation that would not occur without it?

Exclusivity clauses in vendor agreements and SaaS MSAs should be assessed against these factors. Where either party has significant market share, competition law advice should be sought before agreeing to exclusivity.

## 2. Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003

The B-BBEE Act, as amended by Act 46 of 2013, is the primary legislation driving economic transformation in South Africa. It affects commercial contracts through preferential procurement requirements, verification obligations, and the fronting prohibition.

### Scorecard system

B-BBEE compliance is measured against the **Generic Codes of Good Practice** (or sector-specific codes where applicable). The scorecard measures five elements:

| Element | Weighting (Generic Codes) |
|---|---|
| Ownership | 25 points |
| Management control | 19 points |
| Skills development | 20 points |
| Enterprise and supplier development | 40 points |
| Socio-economic development | 5 points |

The total score determines the B-BBEE **recognition level**:

| Level | Score | B-BBEE recognition (procurement) |
|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | 100+ points | 135% |
| Level 2 | 95-99 points | 125% |
| Level 3 | 90-94 points | 110% |
| Level 4 | 80-89 points | 100% |
| Level 5 | 75-79 points | 80% |
| Level 6 | 70-74 points | 60% |
| Level 7 | 55-69 points | 50% |
| Level 8 | 40-54 points | 10% |
| Non-compliant | Below 40 points | 0% |

### EME and QSE thresholds

| Category | Annual turnover | Default level | Verification |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exempt Micro Enterprise (EME) | R10 million or less | Level 4 (Level 1 if 100% black-owned; Level 2 if 51%+ black-owned) | Sworn affidavit or Companies and Intellectual Property Commission certificate |
| Qualifying Small Enterprise (QSE) | R10 million to R50 million | Measured on scorecard | Verification agency assessment |
| Large enterprise | Above R50 million | Measured on scorecard | Verification agency assessment |

### s10 — organs of state procurement

Section 10 requires that organs of state and public entities apply a preferential procurement framework that incorporates B-BBEE. Government buyers must assess tenders using the **Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act** (PPPFA) scoring system:

| Threshold | Scoring |
|---|---|
| Bids up to R50 million | 80/20 (80 points price, 20 points B-BBEE) |
| Bids above R50 million | 90/10 (90 points price, 10 points B-BBEE) |

### Trickle-down to private sector

While B-BBEE is mandatory only for government procurement, the **enterprise and supplier development** element of the scorecard creates a powerful trickle-down effect:

1. Large private sector companies must procure from B-BBEE-compliant suppliers to earn procurement points on their own scorecard.
2. This creates a commercial incentive for vendors to obtain and maintain B-BBEE verification.
3. Many private sector procurement policies mirror the government framework by requiring minimum B-BBEE levels from suppliers.
4. Tender documents in both public and private sectors frequently specify minimum B-BBEE levels as a qualification criterion.

### Impact on commercial contracts

- **Supplier agreements** may require the supplier to maintain a specified minimum B-BBEE level and provide annual verification certificates.
- **Penalties for loss of status** — some contracts include termination rights or price adjustments if the supplier's B-BBEE level drops below the agreed minimum.
- **Subcontracting** — where the primary contract requires a minimum B-BBEE level, the buyer may require the supplier to use B-BBEE-compliant subcontractors.
- **Representation and warranty** — suppliers typically warrant their B-BBEE status and undertake to notify the buyer of any change.

## 3. Fronting prohibition (B-BBEE Act s13F)

### Definition

Fronting is a transaction, arrangement, or other act or conduct that directly or indirectly undermines or frustrates the achievement of the objectives of the B-BBEE Act. Section 13F, introduced by the 2013 amendment, makes fronting a **criminal offence**.

### Indicators of fronting

The B-BBEE Commission has identified the following indicators:

1. **Window-dressing** — black persons appointed in positions but having no substantive role in decision-making, management, or operations.
2. **Benefit diversion** — economic benefits intended for black persons are diverted to non-qualifying persons.
3. **Opportunistic intermediaries** — entities interposed between the buyer and the actual supplier solely to obtain B-BBEE points, without adding genuine value.
4. **Token ownership** — black ownership that is nominal, with no genuine economic interest, voting rights, or participation in profits and losses.
5. **Identity fraud** — misrepresentation of the racial classification of individuals for B-BBEE purposes.

### Penalties

- **Criminal prosecution** — a person convicted of fronting is liable to a fine or imprisonment of up to 10 years, or both.
- **Contract cancellation** — contracts awarded on the basis of false B-BBEE representations can be cancelled.
- **Debarment** — the National Treasury may debar a supplier found guilty of fronting from government contracts for up to 10 years.
- **Verification agency sanctions** — a verification agency that issues a certificate based on false information may have its accreditation revoked.

### High-risk flag: B-BBEE fronting indicators

| Check | What to look for |
|---|---|
| B-BBEE certificate verified | Is the counterparty's B-BBEE certificate current, issued by a SANAS-accredited verification agency? |
| Black persons substantively participating | Do the black shareholders/directors identified in the certificate have genuine decision-making roles? |
| Economic benefits flowing correctly | Are profits, dividends, and economic benefits flowing to the identified black participants? |
| Terms arm's length | Are the commercial terms between the parties (especially where the contract is between related entities) at arm's length? |
| Intermediary adds genuine value | If the contracting entity is an intermediary, does it provide genuine services, or is it interposed solely for B-BBEE scoring? |
| Subcontracting arrangements | Does the prime contractor subcontract substantially all work to a non-compliant entity? |

**Why this matters:** Fronting is a criminal offence since the 2013 amendment. Companies that knowingly benefit from fronting arrangements are exposed to criminal prosecution, contract cancellation, and debarment from government procurement. The B-BBEE Commission has increased investigations and referrals for prosecution.

## 4. Public sector contracting

Contracts with organs of state and public entities are subject to additional requirements beyond standard commercial terms:

### Performance security

Government buyers typically require performance guarantees (bank guarantees or performance bonds) of 5-10% of the contract value. These must be issued by an SA bank or insurer acceptable to the contracting authority.

### Penalty interest rate

Late payment by government entities attracts interest at the **prime rate** as published by the SA Reserve Bank. Some government contracts specify a lower rate, which may be challenged as unfair.

### B-BBEE scoring

B-BBEE status is a formal scoring criterion in government procurement (80/20 or 90/10 as set out above). The supplier's B-BBEE certificate is submitted with the tender and scored accordingly. Misrepresentation of B-BBEE status in a tender is an offence under the PPPFA and may constitute fronting under s13F.

### PFMA and MFMA compliance

Contracts with national government entities must comply with the Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 (PFMA). Contracts with municipalities must comply with the Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA). These Acts impose procurement procedures, approval thresholds, and disclosure requirements that affect contract negotiation timelines and terms.

### National Treasury Instructions

National Treasury issues binding instructions on procurement procedures, including:

- Standard bid documents and contract forms
- Centralised supplier database (CSD) registration requirements
- Tax compliance verification via SARS
- Conflict of interest declarations

## 5. Competition Act interaction with commercial contract terms

### Price provisions

- **Fixed prices** between competitors = per se prohibited (s4(1)(b) price-fixing)
- **Minimum resale prices** imposed by supplier = per se prohibited (s5(2))
- **Maximum resale prices** set by supplier = generally permissible unless they function as fixed prices
- **Recommended resale prices** = permissible if genuinely advisory

### Territory and customer allocation

- Between competitors = per se prohibited market division (s4(1)(b))
- Between supplier and distributor (exclusive territory) = rule of reason under s5(1), considering market share and foreclosure

### Tying and bundling

- Requiring a customer to purchase Product B as a condition of purchasing Product A = potentially a restrictive vertical practice under s5(1) or abuse of dominance under s8 if the supplier has market power

### Information exchange

- Exchanging competitively sensitive information (pricing, capacity, strategy) between competitors = may constitute or facilitate s4 contravention
- Commercial contracts should include safeguards against exchanging competitively sensitive information, particularly in joint venture or consortium arrangements

| US term | SA replacement |
|---|---|
| "Sherman Act" / "Clayton Act" | Competition Act 89 of 1998 |

| "antitrust" | competition law |
| "affirmative action" (US sense) | B-BBEE / economic transformation |
