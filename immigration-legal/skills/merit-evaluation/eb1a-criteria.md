# EB-1A Criteria Reference
## 8 CFR 204.5(h)(3) — Alien of Extraordinary Ability

*Source: 8 CFR 204.5(h)(3). Cross-reference: USCIS Policy Manual, Vol. 6, Part F, Ch. 2.*
*Evidentiary standard: The petitioner must submit evidence that the alien has sustained national or international acclaim and that his or her achievements have been recognized in the field of expertise.*

---

## Filing threshold

The beneficiary must satisfy **at least 3 of the 10 criteria** below (or submit comparable evidence if the listed criteria do not readily apply to the occupation). After meeting the 3-criterion threshold, USCIS conducts a final merits determination under *Kazarian v. USCIS*, 596 F.3d 1115 (9th Cir. 2010), asking whether the totality of the evidence demonstrates sustained acclaim and extraordinary ability.

---

## The 10 criteria

### Criterion 1 — Prizes or Awards
**Regulatory text:** Documentation of the alien's receipt of lesser nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence in the field of endeavor.

**Key interpretive points:**
- "Nationally or internationally recognized" — local or institutional awards alone are typically insufficient unless the award itself carries national recognition.
- Multiple lesser awards can be cumulative.
- Awards from professional associations, government bodies, or international bodies carry more weight than employer-issued recognition.
- Nominee status alone does not satisfy this criterion; receipt of the award is required.
- Policy Manual guidance (6 USCIS-PM F.2): consider the criteria used to grant the award, the significance of the award in the field, the number of awardees, and the limitations on who is eligible to be considered.

**Evidence types:** Award certificates, official letters from awarding bodies, press coverage of the award, comparative evidence of award selectivity.

---

### Criterion 2 — Membership in Associations Requiring Outstanding Achievement
**Regulatory text:** Documentation of the alien's membership in associations in the field for which classification is sought, which require outstanding achievements of their members, as judged by recognized national or international experts.

**Key interpretive points:**
- The association must require outstanding achievements as a *condition of membership* — not merely dues or professional credentials.
- Judged by recognized national or international experts (not a self-assessment).
- Invitation-only societies, academies, and honor societies that vet members based on achievement qualify.
- Ordinary professional memberships (e.g., IEEE, ACM) that require only a degree or employer sponsorship do not satisfy this criterion without evidence of a selective achievement-based vetting process.

**Evidence types:** Association bylaws or membership criteria, invitation letters, expert testimony on selectivity, statistics on acceptance rates.

---

### Criterion 3 — Published Material About the Alien
**Regulatory text:** Published material about the alien in professional or major trade publications or other major media, relating to the alien's work in the field for which classification is sought. Such evidence shall include the title, date, and author of the material, and any necessary translation.

**Key interpretive points:**
- Material must be "about" the alien — incidental mentions in a broader article are insufficient.
- "Professional or major trade publications or other major media" — tier matters; niche or low-circulation outlets require additional evidence of significance.
- Online publications qualify if they meet the "major media" standard.
- Self-authored content (op-eds, LinkedIn, personal websites) does not satisfy this criterion.
- Coverage of the alien's work (a product they built, a discovery they made) counts; purely biographical profiles count; generic industry articles where the alien is quoted once do not.

**Evidence types:** Copies of articles with author, date, and publication, Alexa/SimilarWeb traffic data or circulation figures to establish "major media" tier, evidence that the publication is a recognized trade or professional outlet.

---

### Criterion 4 — Judging the Work of Others
**Regulatory text:** Evidence of the alien's participation, either individually or on a panel, as a judge of the work of others in the same or an allied field of specialization for which classification is sought.

**Key interpretive points:**
- Must involve actual evaluation of others' work — not merely participation in a committee.
- Peer review of journal articles, grant proposal review, competition judging, dissertation committees, and awards panels all qualify.
- Single or limited instances can satisfy this criterion, though frequency strengthens the argument.
- Invited nature of the judging role supports the extraordinary ability claim.

**Evidence types:** Invitations to serve as reviewer, confirmation from journal editors or program chairs, evidence of the journal's or competition's reputation in the field.

---

### Criterion 5 — Original Contributions of Major Significance
**Regulatory text:** Evidence of the alien's original scientific, scholarly, artistic, athletic, or business-related contributions of major significance in the field.

**Key interpretive points:**
- "Original" — the contribution must be the alien's own, not a team effort where the alien's role is unclear.
- "Major significance" is the highest bar in the criteria set. Policy Manual: consider whether the contribution has influenced the field at large, been widely adopted, or is otherwise recognized by experts as significant (beyond ordinary professional or scholarly activity).
- Citation counts alone are often insufficient — USCIS wants evidence that others have *adopted* or *built upon* the contribution.
- Patents, products shipped at scale, widely cited papers combined with expert letters explaining the impact, policy adoption, industry standards incorporating the work — all relevant.
- Expert opinion letters from recognized authorities in the field explaining the significance are critical for this criterion.

**Evidence types:** Expert letters attesting to significance and impact, citation reports with context (not just raw numbers), evidence of adoption in practice (products, standards, policies), patents granted with evidence of licensing or use.

---

### Criterion 6 — Authorship of Scholarly Articles
**Regulatory text:** Evidence of the alien's authorship of scholarly articles in the field, in professional or major trade publications or other major media.

**Key interpretive points:**
- "Scholarly articles" — peer-reviewed journal articles are the clearest fit; conference papers, book chapters, and monographs also qualify with appropriate evidence of the venue's reputation.
- The alien must be an *author* — editorship alone or acknowledgment in others' papers does not satisfy.
- This criterion is about *authorship* (volume and quality of output), not citation impact — though high citation counts strengthen the overall petition.
- Multiple first-author publications in strong journals is the strongest presentation.

**Evidence types:** Copies of articles, journal impact factor or reputation evidence, citation reports (to show field recognition — not required for the criterion but strengthens the overall petition), Google Scholar or similar profile.

---

### Criterion 7 — Display of Work at Artistic Exhibitions or Showcases
**Regulatory text:** Evidence of the display of the alien's work in the field at artistic exhibitions or showcases.

**Key interpretive points:**
- Primarily applicable to visual artists, performers, and designers.
- Venue must be established — major galleries, museums, international festivals, recognized competitions.
- Commercial display (e.g., product design shown at a trade show) can qualify with evidence of the venue's significance.
- Online-only showcases may qualify if the platform is recognized in the field.

**Evidence types:** Exhibition catalogues, invitations, documentation of venue reputation, press coverage of the exhibition.

---

### Criterion 8 — Performance in a Leading or Critical Role
**Regulatory text:** Evidence that the alien has performed in a leading or critical role for organizations or establishments that have a distinguished reputation.

**Key interpretive points:**
- Two elements: (1) leading or critical role and (2) distinguished reputation of the organization.
- "Leading role" — department head, principal investigator, founding team, C-suite. "Critical role" — may be lower in formal hierarchy but the alien's specific contribution was essential to the organization's success.
- Distinguished reputation — not any employer; the organization itself must have a recognized standing in the field.
- Organizational chart evidence, letters from executives attesting to the criticality of the role, and evidence of the organization's reputation all matter.

**Evidence types:** Organizational charts, letters from senior leadership, evidence of the organization's reputation (rankings, press coverage, funding), evidence of the alien's specific contributions and their impact.

---

### Criterion 9 — High Remuneration Relative to Others
**Regulatory text:** Evidence that the alien has commanded a high salary or other significantly high remuneration for services, in relation to others in the field.

**Key interpretive points:**
- "High salary" is relative — must be compared to others in the same field and geography.
- Accepted benchmarks: BLS OES data, employer compensation surveys, Glassdoor or LinkedIn Salary data with careful sourcing, expert letters on compensation norms.
- Stock options, signing bonuses, and deferred compensation can be included with appropriate documentation.
- A salary that is high absolutely but low percentile in a well-compensated field (e.g., tech) will not satisfy.

**Evidence types:** Pay stubs or employment contracts showing compensation, BLS OES tables or comparable benchmark data, expert letters explaining how compensation compares in the field and geography.

---

### Criterion 10 — Commercial Success in the Performing Arts
**Regulatory text:** Evidence of commercial success in the performing arts, as shown by box office receipts or record, cassette, compact disk, or video sales.

**Key interpretive points:**
- Specifically directed at performing arts — musicians, actors, dancers, directors.
- Sales figures, streaming numbers, box office receipts, and comparable commercial metrics all qualify.
- For digital/streaming: certified sales equivalents, playlist followers, view counts on recognized platforms with evidence of industry significance.

**Evidence types:** RIAA certifications, box office reports, streaming analytics, industry rankings or charts.

---

## Comparable evidence

If the above criteria do not readily apply to the beneficiary's occupation (8 CFR 204.5(h)(4)), the petitioner may submit comparable evidence of extraordinary ability. The petitioner must first demonstrate that the listed criteria do not readily apply, then submit evidence that is truly comparable.

*Firm posture on comparable evidence: see `## Evidence standards` → "Comparable evidence" in the practice profile.*

---

## Final merits determination (Kazarian step 2)

After establishing that at least 3 criteria are met, USCIS conducts a holistic review: does the totality of the evidence demonstrate that the beneficiary is among the small percentage at the very top of the field and has sustained national or international acclaim?

The petition letter should directly address this inquiry — it is not enough to list criteria met; the attorney must explain why, taken together, the evidence establishes the beneficiary as extraordinary.
