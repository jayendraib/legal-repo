# EB-2 NIW Criteria Reference
## Matter of Dhanasar, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016)
## National Interest Waiver — Three-Prong Test

*Source: Matter of Dhanasar, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016), which superseded Matter of New York State Dep't of Transportation, 22 I&N Dec. 215 (Act. Assoc. Comm'r 1998). Cross-reference: USCIS Policy Manual, Vol. 6, Part F, Ch. 5.*

---

## Overview

The EB-2 National Interest Waiver waives the job offer and labor certification requirements that ordinarily apply to EB-2 petitions, on the basis that it is in the national interest of the United States to do so. The petitioner must demonstrate **all three prongs**:

1. The proposed endeavor has both **substantial merit** and **national importance**.
2. The beneficiary is **well-positioned to advance** the proposed endeavor.
3. On balance, it would be **beneficial to the United States** to waive the requirements of a job offer and thus of a labor certification.

Unlike EB-1A, there is no minimum criterion count — the three prongs are holistic factors, not a checklist.

---

## Prong 1 — Substantial Merit and National Importance

### Substantial merit

The proposed endeavor must have substantial merit in a recognized field, which can include business, entrepreneurialism, science, technology, culture, health, or education. Merit is judged by the significance of the endeavor itself, not by the beneficiary's role in it.

**Interpretive points:**
- Merit can be established through the field's recognized importance (e.g., cancer research, renewable energy, AI safety) or through specific high-impact applications.
- Commercial viability may support merit but is not required — fundamental research with no immediate commercial application can have substantial merit.
- Petitions in common fields (software engineering, accounting, general medicine) must articulate why the *specific proposed endeavor* has substantial merit, not just the field generally.

**Evidence types:** Expert letters explaining the significance of the research area or business endeavor, government or industry reports establishing national importance of the field, prior funding or recognition of the endeavor.

### National importance

The endeavor must have national importance — its potential impact must extend beyond a local community or individual employer.

**Interpretive points:**
- "National" does not require that the beneficiary work everywhere in the US — it means the *impact* extends nationally or has implications for the nation (e.g., a researcher whose work on antibiotic resistance has national public health implications, even if conducted at a single institution).
- Dhanasar rejected a narrow geographic test in favor of an impact-based test: the question is whether the potential impact has "national implications."
- Policy Manual: USCIS may evaluate prospective impact; the endeavor does not need to have already produced national impact.

**Evidence types:** Documentation of funding from national sources (NSF, NIH, DOE, DARPA), government interest in the field, scope of the proposed work's application, expert letters explaining national implications.

## Prong 2 — Well-Positioned to Advance the Endeavor

The beneficiary must be well-positioned to advance the proposed endeavor. This is the applicant-specific prong — it asks whether *this person* has the skills, knowledge, and record to move the endeavor forward.

### Factors USCIS considers (per Dhanasar and Policy Manual)

- **Education, skills, and knowledge:** Advanced degrees, specialized training, unique expertise relevant to the endeavor.
- **Record of success in related or similar efforts:** Prior projects, publications, products, or achievements that demonstrate the ability to execute.
- **A model or plan for future activities:** A concrete description of what the beneficiary will do in the US and how it advances the endeavor. Vague statements of intent are insufficient.
- **Progress toward achieving the proposed endeavor:** Evidence that the work has already begun and is producing results.
- **Interest from potential customers, users, investors, or other relevant entities:** Letters of support from collaborators, partners, employers, or government agencies who affirm the beneficiary's value.

### Interpretive points

- This prong does not require the beneficiary to be the best in the world — it asks whether they are well-positioned (i.e., have a credible, realistic basis to advance the endeavor).
- For researchers: publications, grants, citations, and expert letters explaining the beneficiary's specific contributions and standing in the field.
- For entrepreneurs: business traction (revenue, users, investment), letters from investors or partners, evidence of market fit.
- For clinicians/practitioners: patient outcomes, program development, relevant credentials, and evidence of impact in their specific area of practice.

**Evidence types:** CV and educational credentials, publication record with citation evidence, grant awards, letters from collaborators or supervisors attesting to the beneficiary's capabilities and contributions, business metrics, product launch evidence.

## Prong 3 — Beneficial to Waive Job Offer and Labor Certification Requirements

This prong asks whether, on balance, it benefits the US to exempt this beneficiary from the normal EB-2 process (job offer + PERM labor certification). It is a balancing test. Dhanasar articulated two considerations:

### (a) The national interest in the endeavor would be adversely affected if a waiver were not granted

In other words: is there urgency, uniqueness, or practical difficulty that makes the normal labor certification process inadequate?

**Interpretive points:**
- If the endeavor is prospective or self-directed (entrepreneur, independent researcher), there is no employer to file a PERM — the waiver is the only viable path, which is itself a basis for the waiver.
- If the beneficiary is an employee, the argument must explain why the labor market test is inappropriate — e.g., the beneficiary's qualifications are so specialized that no US worker is available, or the work is ongoing research that cannot wait for PERM processing.
- USCIS has also credited urgency: time-sensitive research, critical infrastructure work, public health emergencies.

### (b) The beneficiary's qualifications and circumstances suggest they will serve the national interest to a substantially greater degree than would an available US worker

**Interpretive points:**
- This does not require proving no US worker exists (that's the PERM test). It requires showing the beneficiary is sufficiently exceptional that their specific contribution to the national interest justifies the waiver.
- Comparative evidence of the beneficiary's standing relative to peers in the field is helpful.
- Prior government recognition (grants, partnerships with national agencies) supports this sub-prong.

**Evidence types:** Letters from government agencies, national research institutions, or industry bodies expressing interest in the beneficiary's work; evidence that the beneficiary's contributions are unique or not replicable by the general US labor market; documentation of urgency or practical impossibility of the PERM route.

## Proposed endeavor — the keystone of the NIW

Unlike EB-1A (which is backward-looking — what has the beneficiary already achieved?), EB-2 NIW is partially forward-looking — *what will the beneficiary do in the US?*

The petitioner must articulate a proposed endeavor with enough specificity to allow USCIS to evaluate all three prongs:
- What is the work?
- Why does it matter nationally?
- What will the beneficiary specifically do?
- What evidence shows they can do it?
- Why does the national interest require exempting them from the normal process?

A vague proposed endeavor ("continue my research in machine learning") is a common RFE trigger. The petition letter should describe the proposed endeavor as concretely as a grant application: specific problems being solved, methods being used, expected outcomes, and why those outcomes matter.

---

## Common RFE issues

1. **Prong 1:** The endeavor is described too broadly (field = important, specific work = unclear).
2. **Prong 2:** CV submitted but no expert analysis of why the beneficiary's record positions them to succeed.
3. **Prong 3:** No articulation of why the labor certification requirement should be waived — assumed rather than argued.
4. **Proposed endeavor:** Vague description that makes all three prongs harder to argue.
